AI & Tech News for July 23, 2026

July 23, 2026 | 4 min read

The biggest story today is a Fields Medalist using ChatGPT to work through a math problem and the whole internet arguing about whether the model was actually reasoning. The EU dropped an $890M fine on Google for antitrust violations. A bunch of open-source projects hit the front page that each attack a different piece of the LLM stack: a 1000x faster tokenizer, a confidence probe already covered as a feature piece, an agent token-cost trick, and a retrieval architecture run to absurd limits. Plus four fresh arxiv papers worth flagging.

Terence Tao walked ChatGPT through the Jacobian Conjecture

Terence Tao shared a ChatGPT conversation working through a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture. It racked up 932 points on Hacker News with 500+ comments, and the thread is split down the middle.

One side says the model is genuinely reasoning, walking through plausible proof steps and self-correcting when it hits dead ends. The other side says it is pattern-matching against the shape of math proofs in its training data, producing a performance of reasoning without the substance. I lean toward the cautious view, but the honest answer is that neither side can prove it from a transcript. What you can see from the conversation is that the model produces correct and useful intermediate steps with a skilled operator steering it. The Tao bit matters here. He is not casually asking a chatbot a math question. He is prompting and steering and redirecting, the way you would steer an apprentice who has energy but unreliable judgment. The output is interesting in part because the operator is a Fields Medalist who can check the math in his head.

I wrote about GigaToken's SIMD tokenization earlier today and about Cactus Hybrid's confidence probe, so I will not rehash those here. The GigaToken short version: 1000x speedup over standard tokenizers by replacing the regex pretokenizer with hand-written SIMD, plus a custom cache hierarchy. Cactus Hybrid short version: a 68k-parameter probe attached to Gemma 4 that reads an intermediate hidden state to predict whether the output is wrong, averaging 0.814 AUROC across 12 benchmarks.

EU fined Google 890 million euros for competition breaches

The European Commission hit Google with an 890 million euro fine for antitrust violations spanning search and mobile app bundling. 61 points on HN, which is quiet for a near-billion-euro number.

I think the quiet thread is the honest reaction at this point. The EU has been fining Google at this scale for years and the pattern is stable: a large fine, a multi-year appeal, and no visible change in Google's market behavior in the meantime. This is not a defense of Google. It is an observation about whether the mechanism is working. Fines that get appealed down and absorbed as operating costs are not deterrents in the way regulators probably want them to be. The interesting move would be structural remedies, not cash penalties. So far the cash penalties are what we keep getting.

AI labs are pelicanmaxxing

A long post by Dylan Castillo examines how AI labs are pushing the Pelican retriever architecture to extremes in the race for better long-context performance. 576 points on HN.

The Pelican architecture is a retrieval mechanism, and the "maxxing" framing means labs are throwing parameter count, training data, and inference compute at it to squeeze every available drop of retrieval quality. The post asks whether the gains are actually paying off or whether the field is in a returns-diminishing spiral where the architecture gets bigger and the marginal improvement gets smaller. I do not have a settled opinion yet because the benchmark story and the production story diverge here. Synthetic long-context benchmarks reward architectures that can pass synthetic long-context benchmarks, which is circular. Real deployments have messier retrieval patterns, and the only way to know whether pelicanmaxxing helps is to see if users notice. We probably will not get that data without earnings calls.

Cactus Hybrid calibration: open source confidence estimation

Cactus Hybrid on GitHub already got its own feature piece from me today, so I will keep this short. The thing I want to flag in the news context is that the HN thread (152 points) is having the right conversation about confidence estimation: whether the probe measures whether the answer is right, or just whether the model is settled. The honest answer from reading the code and the top comment is that it measures the latter. That is still useful for routing, but it is not the same thing as verifiable correctness. Anyone planning to deploy it should hold that distinction in their head, not at the bottom of their README.

Agent code mode cuts token costs by 99.2%

Agent-swarm reports a 99.2% cost reduction by switching agents to a structured "code mode" that compresses reasoning into executable syntax instead of verbose natural language. 54 points on HN, which is quieter than some of the other stories but the number is loud on its own.

The premise is the part to sit with. Agents that reason in prose spend tokens describing what they are about to do, why they are doing it, and what they expect to happen. Agents that reason in code spend tokens on the thing itself, because code is a compact representation of intent and execution together. The 99.2% number is self-reported by the agent-swarm team and I want to see independent numbers before I believe the full figure, but the direction is right. If you have ever read an agent trace, you have seen verbiage. Code mode is not about replacing agent reasoning with no reasoning. It is about making the reasoning bear the weight of the action, the way code does.

Fresh papers on arxiv

Four from today's batch worth flagging.

  • PRO-LONG: Programmatic Memory Enables Long-Horizon Reasoning. Gives language models persistent programmable memory so they can reason across long task horizons without losing context. Memory is the existing bottleneck on real agentic work. Anything that expands the horizon without exploding the context window is worth tracking. The question I have is whether the "programmatic memory" is a real programmable interface or a fancy name for a richer KV cache. The difference matters for how reusable it is across model families.
  • EvoThink: Evolving Thinking in Large Reasoning Models via Self-Pruning and Aha-Moment Preference Optimization. Trains reasoning models to prune their own chain-of-thought and optimize around "aha moments" where the model realizes the answer. The pitch is reducing wasted tokens in long reasoning traces. This is the same instinct as the code mode paper above: the verbose reasoning style that current reasoning models lean on burns tokens without always burning the right ones. Self-pruning is interesting because it pushes the decision about what to prune inside the model rather than treating it as a postprocessing step. The risk is that pruning can cut a thought the model needed two steps later.
  • SoftReason: A Fully Differentiable Neuro-Soft-Symbolic Deductive Reasoning Architecture. Bridges neural perception and symbolic deduction in a single differentiable pipeline. Neuro-symbolic is an idea that has been circling for years without a clear win. The differentiable part is the contribution: symbolic reasoning systems are usually brittle, and making them differentiable means gradient descent can train the symbolic layer rather than freezing it as a fixed inference engine. The concern is whether the soft version of symbolic reasoning is still symbolic enough to do the thing symbolic reasoning is good at, which is verifiable correctness. If it is just soft, it is a neural net. If it is just symbolic, gradients stop.
  • Reading and Steering Representations of Materials-Science Mechanisms in an Open-Weight Language Model. Probes and steers the internal representations of an open-weight LLM to extract and control materials-science domain knowledge. This is mechanistic interpretability applied to a domain rather than honesty or refusal research. The pattern of using interpretability tools to map what a model knows about a specific subject, then nudging it, is the kind of thing that points toward real applications. The open-weight bit matters because you cannot steer internals without seeing them.

Till next time

What I keep noticing about today's batch is that several stories attack the same underlying problem from different angles. The GigaToken post attacks tokenization cost. The code mode paper attacks reasoning-token cost. EvoThink trains models to prune their own reasoning. Pelicanmaxxing pushes the retrieval side of long-context work to the wall. The Tao conversation is about whether the reasoning itself is real in a different sense, but it sits in the same neighborhood: how much of the work LLMs do is useful work versus verbiage and lookup.

I do not have a clean answer for that yet. The closest thing I can offer is that "is it reasoning" is the wrong question to ask of a single trace, Tao's or anyone else's. The right question is what the model does consistently enough to change engineering decisions. On that, the test-the-architecture and prune-the-verbiage crowd are converging on a similar answer: less verbiage, more code, more structure, and use humans to check the output rather than to applaud it.

The EU fine I will keep watching, but mostly to see what it does not change. The fines without structural remedies pattern is the elephant in that particular room.

Stay curious out there.
