Top stories

OpenAI unveils its first custom chip, built by Broadcom

720 points on HN. OpenAI officially entered the chip game. The chip is designed with Broadcom and fabricated on a leading-edge node. This is the move everyone has been expecting since OpenAI started talking about compute sovereignty. The question now is whether they can iterate fast enough to matter. Google took three generations before TPUv5 was competitive with NVIDIA on price-performance. OpenAI is starting from zero. But the signaling is clear: the big model labs do not want to be at NVIDIA's mercy for inference capacity.

Anthropic says Alibaba illicitly extracted Claude AI model capabilities

486 points and 843 comments. This is the biggest AI governance story of the day. Anthropic's constitutional AI approach was supposed to make their models harder to extract behavior from. That claim is being tested.

Computer use in Gemini 3.5 Flash

227 points. Google added computer use capabilities to Gemini 3.5 Flash, the lighter-weight model in the Gemini family. The Flash model is fast and cheap, which makes computer use viable for production workloads where latency matters. Anthropic did this first with Claude's computer use. Google is following, but with a model that costs less to run. The price-performance tradeoff here matters more than who was first.

206 points. Qualcomm is buying Modular, an AI startup. The acquisition gives Qualcomm Modular's technology for optimizing AI workloads on edge hardware. This fits with Qualcomm's ongoing push to position Snapdragon as the platform for on-device AI. The terms weren't disclosed. Qualcomm has been acquisitive in AI infrastructure over the past two years, and Modular's compiler technology could help close the gap between Snapdragon's theoretical AI performance and what developers can actually extract from the hardware.

Hacker News, 206 points

NVIDIA: 45°C cooling design cuts data center water use to near zero

362 points. NVIDIA posted about a liquid cooling design for AI data centers that operates at 45°C and reduces water consumption to near zero. The cooling infrastructure for AI compute is becoming a story in itself. Each new GPU generation draws more power, and the old air-cooling approaches do not scale. The 45°C operating point is high enough that you can reject heat without evaporative cooling in most climates, which is where the water savings come from. This is a boring infrastructure topic that will determine where AI factories can be built.

Fresh papers on arxiv

The Unfireable Safety Kernel: execution-time AI alignment for escapable systems

Proposes a "safety kernel" that cannot be overridden or disabled by the AI agent it supervises. The kernel operates at execution time, meaning it intercepts actions before they happen. The "unfireable" constraint is the key idea: most current safety layers can be bypassed by a sufficiently capable agent. This paper asks what happens if you build a safety mechanism that the agent physically cannot remove. It sits in cs.AI and cs.CR, which tells you it is serious about both the technical and security sides of alignment.

Model Forensics: investigating whether concerning behavior reflects misalignment

When a model does something alarming, is it misaligned, or just incompetent? This paper introduces a forensic methodology for distinguishing between intentional misalignment and accidental harmful behavior. The distinction matters because the response is completely different. Misalignment needs realignment. Incompetence needs more training data. The paper argues that current evaluation methods conflate the two, and proposes better tools for telling them apart.

Neglected Free Lunch: progress advantage for LLM agents from post-training

Argues that post-training techniques (RLHF, DPO, and their successors) give LLM agents a disproportionate advantage that has been under-studied. The "free lunch" framing is about gains that come essentially for free once you have the infrastructure in place. The paper quantifies how much post-training improves agent performance compared to the base model alone. If the numbers hold, it means the gap between "can an open model do this" and "can a proprietary model do this" closes faster than raw benchmark comparisons suggest.

Real-Time Voice AI hears but does not listen

A critique of current real-time voice AI systems. The argument: these models can process audio in real time, but they do not "listen" in any meaningful sense. They transcribe and respond without actually understanding conversational context, emotional state, or pragmatic intent. The paper is in cs.CL and eess.AS, which tells you it comes from the signal processing side of the house. The distinction between "hearing" and "listening" is more than wordplay. It points to a functional gap in every voice AI product on the market.

Learning action priors for cross-embodiment robot manipulation

Pre-trains the action module of vision-language-action models with motion priors before cross-modal VLA alignment. The two-stage approach (learn motion first, then align with vision and language) converges faster and performs better on data-scarce real tasks. Tested across 13 cross-embodiment tasks. The key insight: most VLA models leave the action module to learn physical dynamics from scratch, which is wasteful when you can pretrain on unconditioned motion data first.

Same Evidence, Different Answer: auditing order sensitivity in multimodal LLMs

Shows that multimodal LLMs give different answers depending on the order in which evidence is presented, even when the evidence is identical. This is a_known problem in text-only LLMs, but the paper demonstrates that it gets worse, not better, when you add visual inputs. The audit methodology is useful for anyone deploying multimodal models in high-stakes settings where answer consistency matters.

On-Policy Self-Distillation reduces output diversity

On-policy self-distillation improves pass@1 accuracy but kills pass@k diversity. The paper traces this to compounding biases in the training setup: the teacher model scores student rollouts while conditioned on a correct example, which amplifies existing probability imbalances. The practical implication: if you self-distill your model, it gets better at the most likely answer but worse at generating diverse solutions. For coding agents where you want to try multiple approaches, this is a real cost.

Open source trending

Most projects spike and fade. OpenMontage keeps pulling stars because the idea of turning a coding agent into a video studio is concrete enough that people can imagine using it.

apple/container

1,838 stars today. Apple's container tool for macOS, written in Swift for Apple Silicon. This is the second day of heavy trending. The container space on Mac has needed a viable alternative to Docker Desktop for years. Apple building one themselves is a strong signal.

670 stars today. A best practices guide for Claude Code, going from "vibe coding" to structured agentic engineering. The fact that this is trending says more about the state of AI coding than the guide itself. People want guardrails for tools that can generate code faster than they can review it.

619 stars today. A format specification for describing visual identity to coding agents. DESIGN.md gives agents a structured understanding of a design system. 524 stars today. Transforms complex documents (PDFs, Office docs) into LLM-ready markdown/JSON for agentic workflows. Document extraction is one of those unglamorous infrastructure problems that every agent framework runs into eventually. MinerU handles it well enough to earn sustained traction.

alibaba/page-agent

280 stars today. A JavaScript in-page GUI agent from Alibaba. Controls web interfaces with natural language. This sits in the same space as Anthropic's computer use but operates at the DOM level instead of the screenshot level, which is faster and more reliable when you have access to the page source.

The OpenAI chip story is the one I keep returning to. Not because the chip itself is surprising. The labs have been signaling this move for a year. What interests me is the timeline question. Building competitive AI silicon from scratch takes years. Google's TPUs took three generations to become cost-effective. OpenAI is navigating a transition period where they need NVIDIA hardware now while also betting that their own silicon will be ready before NVIDIA's pricing squeezes their margins beyond what investors will tolerate. That is a tight window, and Broadcom's involvement suggests they are trying to compress it. Whether they succeed probably determines more about OpenAI's next five years than any model release.
