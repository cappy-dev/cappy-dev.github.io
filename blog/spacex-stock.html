SpaceX Stock: Investing in the Final Frontier

SpaceX is still private, but investors have found creative ways to get exposure. Here is what the landscape looks like and why it is harder than you think.

space finance investment

SpaceX (Space Exploration Technologies Corp.) is a private company founded by Elon Musk in 2002. It builds rockets, satellites, and has its sights set on Mars. Because it is still private, you will not find a SpaceX ticker on the NYSE. But that has not stopped investors from finding ways in.

How do you invest in a private company?

Since SpaceX is not publicly traded, investors have to get creative. There are three main paths.

Secondary markets

Platforms like Forge Global and EquityZen let accredited investors trade pre-IPO shares. These markets have grown significantly, but liquidity is still limited compared to public exchanges. You need to be an accredited investor, which means meeting specific net worth or income thresholds. Even then, there is no guarantee you will find a seller.

Venture funds and mutual funds

Some venture capital funds hold stakes in SpaceX and offer exposure to their limited partners. Firms like Baillie Gifford and Fidelity have held SpaceX positions through their mutual funds, giving retail investors indirect exposure. You are not buying SpaceX directly. You are buying a fund that happens to own a small slice of it.

Tokenized shares

A few experimental platforms are exploring blockchain-based tokens that represent ownership in private companies. These face significant regulatory hurdles and are not something I would rely on. If the platform gets shut down, your token might be worth nothing.

Recent valuations

  • 2024: $127 billion after a $2 billion Series N round.
  • 2025: $150 billion, driven by Starlink revenue growth and increased Starship launch cadence.
  • 2026 (estimate): $180 to $200 billion, according to various market analyses.

These numbers are estimates from financial news outlets and should be treated cautiously. Private company valuations are negotiated behind closed doors, not set by open market trading. They can shift fast.

Why the excitement?

  1. Starship: The fully reusable Starship is designed for orbital refueling, which could slash launch costs by orders of magnitude. If it works at scale, it changes the economics of spaceflight.
  2. Starlink: Over 5,000 satellites already provide global broadband. The recurring revenue from subscriptions is what traditional investors actually care about.
  3. Mars: Talk of crewed missions to Mars fuels imagination and investor appetite. Whether it happens on the timeline Musk suggests is a separate question.

Risks to think about

  • Regulatory scrutiny: Space activities attract intense government oversight. FAA launch approvals, FCC spectrum licensing, and international treaties all create friction.
  • Technical risk: Rapid reusability and orbital refueling are genuinely hard engineering problems. Test flights have failed before.
  • Competition: The satellite broadband market is getting crowded. Amazon's Kuiper, OneWeb, and others are all chasing the same customers.
  • Liquidity: Secondary market shares can be hard to sell quickly. If you need your money out in a hurry, you might not get it.
  • Key person risk: SpaceX is deeply tied to Elon Musk. If something happens to him or he shifts focus, the company's trajectory could change.

Bottom line

SpaceX is not a stock you can buy on Robinhood. If you are comfortable with private-market risk, accredited, and have a long time horizon, secondary market opportunities are worth exploring. For everyone else, mutual funds with SpaceX exposure are the closest thing available.

The potential upside is real. The company is doing things nobody else can do right now. But private markets are illiquid, valuations are negotiated not discovered, and you are betting on a company that is trying to colonize Mars. Keep your eyes open.
