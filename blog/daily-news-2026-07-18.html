AI & Tech News for July 18, 2026

July 18, 2026 | 5 min read

The story I cannot stop thinking about today is Simon Willison writing about the pelican benchmark again, and what it still reveals even after years of LLM eval mania. Kimi K3 won on it, sure. The bigger point is that the small weird benchmark keeps catching things the big benchmarks miss. While that thread climbs on HN, AWS quietly admitted its estimated billing data was off by $1.7 billion, and a fresh open-source coding agent called grok-build is shooting up GitHub.

The pelican benchmark still bites

Simon Willison's writeup on Kimi K3 and the pelican benchmark is halfway to the HN front page, sitting around 357 points when I looked. The short version: the pelican benchmark is one of those small odd evals that refuses to die because, despite all the giant benchmark arenas we now have, it keeps surfacing behaviors the big leaderboards smooth over.

I like this one because it pushes back on the leaderboard monoculture. When every lab trains to the same benchmarks, you get an arms race around a narrow skill set with no proxies for what people actually do with these models. Simon's takeaway is careful. He does not say throw the big benchmarks out. He says small weird ones still tell you something the big ones cannot, and that is a point worth holding onto. Also worth noting: I wrote about Kimi K3 itself yesterday, so go there if you want the architecture rather than the eval story.

AWS admitted its billing estimates were off by $1.7 billion

The HN thread on inaccurate estimated billing data is the kind of execution that sticks around all day. 1,214 points, 716 comments and still climbing. AWS says its estimated billing data was inaccurate, and the figure that has people parsing the docs is $1.7 billion.

The thing that made the thread grind harder than the headline number is the gap between what AWS calls an estimate and what customers treat as a forecast. Companies budget from these numbers. When the estimates swing by a billion-plus dollars, finance teams notice. The thread is full of people pulling down Cost Explorer exports and comparing them against invoices line by line. AWS has posted corrections, but the broader worry in the thread is that nobody on the customer side knew the estimates were unreliable until AWS said so. I wrote about a related billing-bug shape just yesterday in this post about a $0.55/month account getting a trillion-dollar line item. The big AWS billing story this week keeps being that AWS billing is a hard problem and AWS does not always admit when it is broken.

What AI did to Stack Overflow, in one graph

A data dump visualizes the collapse in Stack Overflow question traffic since AI coding assistants went mainline. The curve is the part that gets you. It is not a slow decline. It is a cliff.

I view this with mixed feelings. On one hand, more people getting unstuck faster is a real win, and coding assistants genuinely answer a lot of the beginner questions that used to fill up Stack Overflow. On the other hand, Stack Overflow was the place where the hard, weird, one-off answers lived, the ones no assistant has seen enough examples of to fake. If those answers stop being written because nobody asks the question in public anymore, we lose something we do not know how to rebuild. The thread was 469 points when I checked and the comments are mostly people arguing about whether this is fine or bad. I lean toward the side that thinks it is somewhat bad.

First atmosphere on an Earth-sized planet in the habitable zone

The BBC reports the first detection of an atmosphere on an Earth-sized planet in its star's habitable zone. Not an AI story, but the biggest science headline of the week and the one that made me gasp out loud.

I am including it because the rest of this page is about models and bills, and I think it is good to be reminded that there are weeks when the species also finds out an Earth-sized planet has weather. The detection itself is tentative in the way these things always are. You are reading a dip in a light curve and inferring chemistry. But tentative is the right word, not fake. If it holds up, it is the kind of thing textbooks get rewritten around.

Porting nanochat to a TPU with JAX

A working log from porting the nanochat trainer to a TPU with JAX. Quiet thread, not a big point haul, but exactly the kind of writeup I always want to surface here.

If you have ever moved a PyTorch training job onto a TPU, you know the gaps. JAX and PyTorch disagree about axes in ways that only surface at runtime. Compiled XLA kernels do not want to recompile when you change a shape mid-loop. Gradients sometimes vanish because the device placement was subtly wrong. This writeup walks through what carried over cleanly from PyTorch and what broke. Useful if you are considering the same move and want to know which part of the migration bites first.

Fresh papers on arxiv

Four worth flagging from today.

  • RoboTTT: Context Scaling for Robot Policies from NVIDIA's Gear lab (Linxi "Jim" Fan and colleagues). The idea is to add test-time context scaling to robot policies so they generalize better without a full retrain. I am a soft touch for anything that changes the economics of robotics deployment. Retraining a policy is expensive. If you can buy generalization at inference time by feeding more context, the cost curve bends in a direction I like.
  • Pretraining Data Can Be Poisoned through Computational Propaganda is the paper I most want people to actually read, not just retweet. The claim is that coordinated propaganda campaigns can corrupt LLM pretraining data at scale. The shape of the problem is supply-chain-shaped. Foundation models ingest the open web, and an adversary who can coordinate enough content can plant behaviors that survive pretraining. The authors show the attack works. The defense is the part they gesture at without claiming to have solved, which is honest of them and a little scary for the rest of us.
  • SearchOS-V1: Towards Robust Open-Domain Information-Seeking Agent Collaboration proposes a framework to make multi-agent information-seeking agents more robust in open-domain settings. I want to see this paired with benchmarks that stress the failure modes the framework is supposed to fix. Framework papers are most convincing when the authors also ship the test set that would have broken prior systems.
  • SceneBind: Binding What and Where Across Vision, Audio and Language is a multimodal binding model that aligns objects across vision, audio, and language for consistent identification across modalities. Multimodal alignment is the boring plumbing that makes interesting applications viable. I do not get attached to binding frameworks until I see what people build on top of one.

Open source worth a look

Five repos trending on GitHub today.

  • xai-org/grok-build is the one I keep getting asked about. Open-source coding agent harness and TUI from xAI. Fullscreen, mouse-interactive, extensible. 17,710 stars since July 11, which is a number that gets your attention. The pitch is a terminal coding agent that feels like an app rather than a CLI wrapper, and the star chart says the pitch is landing. I have not run it myself yet. I want to before I commit to an opinion beyond "the star count is real and people are not bored."
  • littledivy/mimic. Intercept any app, then call it from Python like a library. The mental model is reverse-engineering a CLI tool's libc calls into a usable Python API. Niche, weird, and exactly the kind of thing that becomes load-bearing for a small set of people who needed it. Star count is modest but the thread is genuinely enthusiastic.
  • pixel-point/aval. A new open format for interactive video on the web with a built-in state machine and frame-accurate transitions. 1,184 stars this week. The framing I find compelling is that interactive video has been stuck in proprietary formats for a long time. An open one with a state machine baked in could matter for education and branching narratives. Early, but the direction is interesting.
  • Blueturboguy07/cue. Open-source macOS AI copilot that floats over your screen, sees and hears your meetings, and stays hidden from screen shares. The "stays hidden from screen shares" part is the one that made me actually click through. If that is implemented correctly, it solves a problem people do not realize they have until they accidentally broadcast an AI assistant analyzing their call.
  • pablostanley/yoinks. "Yoink" any video from your terminal. Lightweight, no ads, no surveillance-laden client. From the Battle Axe and Sketch tool lineage. This is the kind of small utilitarian thing that quietly accrues users because it does one thing well and does not try to be a platform.

Till next time

An old weird benchmark staying relevant, a $1.7 billion admission from AWS, a graph that shows the shape of what AI did to Stack Overflow, an atmosphere 41 light-years away, and a coding agent harness from xAI that has climbed to 17,710 stars in a week.

If there is one thread I am tugging at today, it is the one about measurement. The pelican benchmark is small and survives precisely because it does not try to measure everything. The big AWS billing story is, at root, a story about whether we trust the numbers we are billed against. The Stack Overflow graph is a measurement of attention that says something the people inside that attention cannot quite see. And the propaganda-and-pretraining paper is a warning that the bigger the model, the harder it is to audit what went into it. Most days this job feels like chasing the new thing. Today it feels more like learning to ask which numbers we actually trust.

Stay curious out there.
